Lifetime ROI on your degree

How the ROI calculation works

This calculator estimates the financial return on your degree by comparing lifetime earnings with a degree versus without one, then subtracting the total cost of the degree including loan interest. A positive ROI means your degree is expected to pay for itself financially over your working career. A higher ROI means a faster payback and greater long-term financial advantage.

The calculation uses your expected starting salary, an assumed annual salary growth rate, the salary you would likely earn without a degree, your total degree cost, and your loan details. The longer your career and the higher your earnings premium, the better your ROI.

Average earnings premium for bachelor's degree holders

On average, bachelor's degree holders earn about $1.2 million more over their lifetimes than high school graduates. But that number represents a wide distribution — it is not evenly spread across all majors. Engineering and computer science graduates see the largest earnings premiums. Arts, humanities, and social science graduates see smaller premiums, though they still typically benefit financially from having a degree.

School cost matters as much as major

Two students with identical majors and starting salaries can have very different ROIs based on how much their degrees cost. A $60,000 total degree cost versus a $200,000 total degree cost, all else being equal, produces dramatically different payback timelines. The degree market generally does not reward the cost premium of expensive schools outside a narrow set of elite programs.

Non-financial returns

This calculator only measures financial ROI. Degrees also provide professional networks, career flexibility, access to graduate programs, and personal development that have real value but are difficult to quantify. Use this calculator as one input in your decision, not the only one.
