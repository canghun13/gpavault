How the ACT is scored

The ACT consists of four sections: English (75 questions, 45 minutes), Mathematics (60 questions, 60 minutes), Reading (40 questions, 35 minutes), and Science (40 questions, 35 minutes). Each section is scored on a scale of 1 to 36. Your composite score is the average of all four section scores, rounded to the nearest whole number.

An optional Writing section is scored separately on a scale of 2 to 12 and does not affect your composite score. Some colleges require the Writing section, so check requirements for your target schools before registering.

What ACT score do you need?

Score requirements vary widely by school. A composite of 20 or above is generally considered at or above average. Most four-year colleges accept students in the 18–28 range. Selective schools typically look for 30 or above. Highly selective schools (top 25 nationally) tend to have middle 50% ranges of 33–36.

A low section score can bring down your composite significantly. If one section is notably weaker than others, targeted prep for that section often produces the biggest composite gains.

ACT vs SAT: which to take?

Both are accepted by virtually all four-year colleges in the United States. Some students perform better on one test than the other — the ACT has a heavier science section and slightly faster pacing, while the SAT has more reading comprehension and data analysis. Taking a free practice test for each is the best way to find out which suits you.
