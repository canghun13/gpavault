Debt-to-income ratio

The 1x rule: debt should not exceed starting salary

The most widely cited benchmark for manageable student loan debt: your total balance at graduation should not exceed your expected first-year salary. If you plan to earn $55,000, try to keep total debt under $55,000. On a standard 10-year repayment plan, this produces a monthly payment of roughly 1% of your balance — challenging but workable.

Exceed this ratio significantly and loan payments can consume a large enough share of monthly income to delay saving for retirement, buying a home, or building any financial cushion.

The 10% monthly payment rule

Your monthly student loan payment ideally should not exceed 10% of your gross monthly income. Above that threshold, loans begin to crowd out other essential expenses. Calculate your expected monthly payment using the standard formula, then compare it to your projected monthly gross income. If the ratio exceeds 10%, you are borrowing more than is comfortably serviceable at that income level.

What happens when you exceed these thresholds?

Exceeding the 1x or 10% benchmarks does not mean you cannot manage — but it does mean you will likely need an income-driven repayment plan to keep monthly payments affordable. Income-driven plans reduce monthly payments but extend your loan term and increase total interest paid. For borrowers in public service roles, this may be the right call because PSLF forgiveness after 10 years changes the math entirely.
