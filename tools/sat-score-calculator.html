How the SAT is scored

The SAT has two sections: Evidence-Based Reading and Writing (EBRW) and Math. Each section is scored from 200 to 800, for a combined total of 400 to 1600. The national average composite score is approximately 1010. There is no penalty for guessing — always answer every question.

What score do you need?

Score competitiveness depends entirely on your target schools. Most four-year colleges admit students across a wide range. For broad access, a score of 1000 or above is a reasonable baseline. For selective schools, 1300 or above is generally competitive. For highly selective schools in the top 25 nationally, the middle 50% of admitted students typically falls between 1450 and 1570.

Look up the middle 50% score range for each school you are applying to — this is published on every college's Common Data Set and gives you a realistic sense of where you stand.

SAT section scores and their meaning

Admissions offices look at both your total score and your section scores. STEM-focused programs often weigh the Math section more heavily. Strong writing programs may look closely at EBRW. If one section is significantly lower than the other, targeted prep in that area tends to produce the fastest composite gains.

SAT vs ACT

Both tests are accepted equally by essentially all U.S. colleges. The SAT emphasizes reading comprehension, data interpretation, and extended math problems. The ACT includes a dedicated Science section and moves at a faster pace. Taking a free practice test for each is the most reliable way to determine which format plays to your strengths.
