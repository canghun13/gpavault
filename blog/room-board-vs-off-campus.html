Room and Board vs Off-Campus Living: What's Actually Cheaper in College?

7 min read · May 2026
Advertisement · Google AdSense

The average on-campus room and board runs $11,500 to $14,000 per year at public universities. Private schools often charge $15,000 to $18,000. Those numbers sound high — until you start pricing out what off-campus actually costs when you account for everything.

The short answer is: it depends on where you go to school. This guide walks through a real cost comparison so you can make the right call for your situation.

What on-campus room and board actually includes

On-campus housing bundles several costs that students forget to count when comparing to off-campus. Your room and board fee typically covers:

  • Housing (shared double or single room in a residence hall)
  • A meal plan — usually 14 to 19 meals per week at a dining hall
  • All utilities: electricity, heat, water, and high-speed WiFi
  • Building maintenance and security
  • Zero commute cost — you walk to class

When students say dorms are expensive, they are often comparing the sticker price to just the rent portion of off-campus housing, ignoring food, utilities, and transportation. That is not an apples-to-apples comparison.

When on-campus is the better deal

On-campus housing wins financially in several common situations:

  • High cost-of-living cities. In NYC, San Francisco, Boston, Chicago, and similar metros, off-campus rents can easily exceed what your school charges for room and board — especially once you add utilities and food.
  • Freshman year. The bundled cost often compares well, and you avoid setup costs like furniture, deposits, and household supplies.
  • When financial aid covers it. If your aid package includes a room and board allowance that matches or exceeds on-campus costs, living on campus uses your aid efficiently.
  • When you do not own a car. Off-campus living often requires transportation costs that add up quickly if your school is not walkable.

When off-campus is the better deal

Off-campus can save meaningful money when conditions are right:

  • College towns with low rents. In smaller markets, splitting a 3-bedroom house with two roommates can come to $350 to $500 per person per month — well below on-campus costs.
  • Three or more roommates. More roommates means the fixed costs of rent and utilities are divided further. Four people in a 4-bedroom can cut housing costs dramatically.
  • Junior and senior year. By then you know how to cook, budget, and manage utilities — the learning curve is behind you.
  • Opting out of the meal plan. If your school lets upperclassmen live off-campus and skip the mandatory meal plan, cooking your own food can save $200 to $400 per month compared to a dining plan.

The hidden cost nobody talks about: time

A 20-minute one-way commute adds up to roughly 40 hours of travel per semester — a full work week. For students who are also working part-time or carrying a heavy course load, that time has real value. Living close to campus can mean better grades, more sleep, and more time for everything else.

This is not a financial calculation, but it is a real cost. Factor it in when making your decision.

How to do the real comparison for your school

The steps are simple. First, find your school's published room and board cost — this is on the financial aid or housing page of your school's website. Then research actual rental prices near campus using Zillow, Apartments.com, or Facebook Marketplace. Add in realistic estimates for utilities, food, and transportation. Compare the two totals, not just the rent line.

Many students are surprised to find that on-campus housing is competitive once the full comparison is done. Others find they can save $3,000 to $5,000 per year by moving off-campus. The answer is genuinely different depending on your school and market.
