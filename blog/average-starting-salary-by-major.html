Average Starting Salary by Major (2026): Is Your Degree Paying Off?

Choosing a major is one of the biggest financial decisions you will ever make. Yet most students pick based on interest alone, without knowing what graduates in their field actually earn. That gap between expectation and reality is where student loan debt becomes unmanageable.

This guide breaks down 2026 starting salary data by major category, explains what drives the variation, and helps you figure out whether your debt load is proportionate to your expected income.

STEM majors

STEM fields consistently produce the highest starting salaries, driven by strong employer demand and a relatively limited supply of qualified graduates. Computer science and software engineering in particular have seen sustained salary growth over the past decade.

Note that biology and life sciences sit significantly below the other STEM fields. Many biology graduates go on to medical school, pharmacy school, or graduate research, which changes the long-term earnings picture considerably — but also means more years of training and often more debt.

Business and Finance

Business majors cover a wide range of career paths and salary outcomes. Finance and economics skew higher because graduates frequently enter competitive industries like investment banking, consulting, and corporate finance. Marketing and management salaries depend heavily on the employer, industry, and location.

Business degrees from top programs (think Wharton, Ross, Booth) can open doors to significantly higher salaries. But for most regional or state school programs, the salary ranges above are realistic benchmarks.

Health and Human Services

Healthcare majors offer strong job security and solid salaries, especially nursing. Social work and psychology tend to have lower starting salaries but provide meaningful career tracks with room for advancement through licensure and specialization.

MajorMedian Starting SalaryMid-Career Median

Psychology$38,000–$48,000$55,000–$75,000

Psychology is a special case. A bachelor's degree in psychology alone has modest earning potential. The field rewards graduate degrees significantly — clinical psychologists and licensed counselors with master's or doctoral degrees earn far more, but the additional training adds years and cost.

Liberal Arts and Humanities

Liberal arts graduates have lower starting salaries on average, but the data tells a more complicated story. Many liberal arts majors end up in fields not directly related to their degree — law, government, nonprofit, media, and business roles — and their long-term earnings vary accordingly.

What these numbers don't tell you

Starting salary is a snapshot. Career trajectory matters enormously over a 40-year working life. A social worker earning $42,000 today can build toward licensure, management, and specialized roles. An engineer earning $85,000 can accelerate into six-figure territory within a few years.

Geography is another major variable. A $55,000 salary in rural Ohio is comfortable. The same salary in San Francisco barely covers rent. When comparing majors, try to factor in where you plan to live and work, not just the national average.

The debt-to-salary rule

A widely used benchmark: your total student loan debt at graduation should not exceed your expected first-year salary. If you plan to earn $50,000, try to keep total debt under $50,000. Go into $120,000 of debt for a $40,000/year job, and you will spend most of your twenties paying it down.
