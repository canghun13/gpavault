Is a College Degree Worth the Debt? How to Calculate Your ROI

8 min read · May 2026
Advertisement · Google AdSense

On average, a bachelor's degree holder earns roughly $1.2 million more over a lifetime than a high school graduate. That number gets cited constantly — but it hides enormous variation by major, school, and career path. For some students, a degree is the best financial decision they will ever make. For others, borrowing $120,000 for a $38,000/year job is a math problem with a bad answer.

This guide shows you how to run the ROI calculation for your specific situation, not the average.

The basic ROI formula

Return on investment for a degree can be estimated as:

(Lifetime earnings premium − total cost of degree) ÷ total cost of degree × 100 = ROI %

The earnings premium is the difference between what you would earn with a degree versus without one, over your working career. The total cost includes tuition, fees, room and board, and the opportunity cost of not working full-time for four years.

This sounds complicated, but the simplified version is practical: how long does it take for your higher salary to pay off the debt and break even? That payback period tells you a lot.

A computer science graduate borrowing $60,000 and earning $90,000 in their first year has a very different financial picture than an education major borrowing $80,000 and earning $42,000. The degree is the same credential type — the ROI is completely different.

School cost matters as much as major

This is the part most students do not fully reckon with. A computer science degree from a $15,000/year state school costs about $60,000 total. The same degree from a $60,000/year private university costs $240,000. Employers paying $90,000 for a CS graduate generally do not pay four times more for the private school version.

Outside of a small number of elite programs (top-10 schools where the brand genuinely opens specific doors), the market does not consistently reward the prestige premium. You are usually better off graduating with less debt from a solid state school than with massive debt from a name-brand private one.

The debt ceiling that breaks the math

There is a rough threshold where degree ROI turns negative or at minimum becomes very difficult to manage: when total student loan debt exceeds 1.5 times your expected starting salary. Beyond that ratio, loan payments eat so much of your monthly income that wealth accumulation is severely delayed.

Here is what that looks like in practice:

  • $60,000 debt, $85,000 starting salary → manageable, ROI positive
  • $80,000 debt, $55,000 starting salary → tight but workable with income-driven repayment
  • $120,000 debt, $40,000 starting salary → monthly payment exceeds 20% of gross income, financially very difficult
  • $180,000 debt, $42,000 starting salary → payment-to-income ratio is unsustainable without forgiveness programs

Non-financial value is real but not a blank check

Degree ROI calculations miss important things: career flexibility, professional networks, doors that only open with a credential (medical school, law school, many government roles), and the genuine intellectual value of higher education. For many careers, the degree is not optional — the ROI question is which degree at what cost.

That said, "college is valuable" is not a reason to borrow without limits. The non-financial value of a degree does not pay your loan balance. Be clear-eyed about both sides.

How to calculate your own ROI

Start with three numbers: your expected starting salary for your major and career path (research this specifically — don't rely on averages), your projected total loan balance at graduation, and your school's total cost per year. Then use the degree ROI calculator to see your payback period and lifetime return.

If the math is bad, it is better to know before you commit — not after you graduate.
