Student Loan Repayment Plans Explained: Standard, IBR, RAP (2026)

12 min read · Updated June 2026
Advertisement · Google AdSense

The federal student loan repayment landscape changed significantly in 2026. The SAVE plan, which replaced REPAYE in 2023, has itself been replaced by the Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) starting July 1, 2026. If you have federal student loans, the plan you are on directly affects your monthly payment, total interest paid, and eligibility for loan forgiveness. Here is every plan explained clearly, and how to decide which one fits your situation.

Standard Plan: the financially optimal choice if you can afford it

The Standard Plan divides your balance into 120 equal monthly payments over 10 years. No income verification, no annual recertification, no complexity. You pay the same amount every month for 10 years and the loan is gone.

It minimizes total interest paid because you eliminate principal faster. Here is the real cost comparison:

One important note: Standard Plan payments count toward PSLF. If you are pursuing PSLF and can afford the standard payment, you could have your entire remaining balance forgiven after 10 years — which makes paying extra beyond the minimum counterproductive.

Graduated Plan: lower start, higher finish

Graduated payments start lower and increase every two years, completing repayment in 10 years. Designed for borrowers who expect their income to grow significantly in the early years. Total interest paid is higher than the Standard Plan (because early payments are smaller and more interest accumulates), but the graduated structure can ease cash flow pressure in the first few years post-graduation.

IBR (Income-Based Repayment): the core safety net plan

IBR caps your monthly payment based on your income and family size. For new borrowers (after July 1, 2014), the cap is 10% of discretionary income. For older loans, it is 15%. Discretionary income is roughly your adjusted gross income minus 150% of the federal poverty guideline for your family size.

What this means in practice: if your income is low enough, your IBR payment can be very low — potentially zero. Your loan does not default just because you pay $0; you remain in good standing. Interest continues to accrue, but IBR's interest protections prevent your balance from growing in many cases.

The trade-off: the loan term extends to 20 or 25 years, and any remaining balance at the end is forgiven (though that forgiven amount may be taxable income under current law). For borrowers with high debt-to-income ratios, IBR often provides necessary financial breathing room.

IBR is the most flexible income-driven option for most borrowers and the most common plan for PSLF candidates. You must recertify your income annually — missing recertification is the most common IBR mistake and can cause your payment to spike.

PAYE (Pay As You Earn)

PAYE caps payments at 10% of discretionary income (same as new-borrower IBR) with a 20-year forgiveness timeline. To qualify, you must be a new borrower as of October 1, 2007 with a Direct Loan disbursed after October 1, 2011, and your payment under PAYE must be lower than your Standard Plan payment.

PAYE has one advantage over IBR for some borrowers: the 20-year forgiveness timeline applies regardless of whether your loans were graduate or undergraduate. IBR requires 25 years if you have any graduate school loans. For borrowers with graduate debt who do not qualify for PSLF, this distinction can matter.

RAP: The new plan replacing SAVE (July 1, 2026)

The Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) replaces SAVE as of July 1, 2026. Key features:

  • Payment structure: Tiered percentages of your full adjusted gross income (AGI), ranging from 1% to 10% depending on income bracket. Unlike SAVE and IBR, which use discretionary income, RAP uses total AGI — a different calculation that produces different payments depending on your income.
  • Minimum payment: $10 per month. RAP eliminates the $0 payment option that SAVE provided for very low-income borrowers.
  • Interest protection: Like SAVE, unpaid interest does not capitalize under RAP — your balance does not grow when your payment does not cover accruing interest.
  • Forgiveness timeline: 30 years, which is longer than SAVE's 20-year undergraduate forgiveness and PAYE's 20-year timeline.
  • PSLF compatibility: RAP payments count toward Public Service Loan Forgiveness.

For borrowers who previously had very low or $0 SAVE payments, RAP's $10 minimum is a change. For most borrowers with moderate incomes, the practical effect depends on whether the AGI-based calculation produces a higher or lower payment than their old discretionary income calculation. PSLF: the most valuable program most people underuse

Public Service Loan Forgiveness forgives your entire remaining federal Direct Loan balance after 120 qualifying payments (10 years) while working full-time for a government or qualifying nonprofit employer. The forgiven balance is currently tax-free, and there is no dollar cap on forgiveness.

To qualify, every element must be in place:

  • Direct Loans only (FFEL and Perkins loans do not qualify without consolidation)
  • On an income-driven repayment plan (IBR, PAYE, RAP, or Standard Plan)
  • Full-time employment at a qualifying employer (government, 501(c)(3) nonprofit)
  • 120 qualifying payments — these do not need to be consecutive

Submit an Employment Certification Form (now called the PSLF Form) every year, not just at year 10. Annual submission allows problems to be identified and corrected while you still have time to fix them. Borrowers who wait until year 10 to submit sometimes discover years of payments did not qualify — a preventable mistake.

For teachers, nurses, social workers, government employees, and nonprofit workers with significant loan balances, PSLF can represent six-figure forgiveness. It fundamentally changes the repayment math and should inform which plan you choose from day one.

How to choose the right plan

The decision depends on three factors: your income relative to your debt, your employer type, and your timeline.

  • If you can afford standard payments comfortably: Standard Plan is almost always the best financial choice. You pay the least total interest and the loan is gone in 10 years.
  • If your payment-to-income ratio is high (payment above 10% of gross monthly income): An income-driven plan (IBR or RAP) reduces monthly burden and keeps you in good standing while your income grows.
  • If you work in public service or nonprofit: IBR or RAP plus PSLF is often the optimal strategy — even if you could technically afford standard payments. Every dollar of remaining balance after 120 qualifying payments is forgiven tax-free. Paying extra beyond the minimum reduces your forgiveness benefit.
  • If you have high graduate school debt and no PSLF path: PAYE's 20-year forgiveness timeline may be better than IBR's 25-year timeline for graduate debt.
