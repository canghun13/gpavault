Student Loan Repayment Plans Explained: Standard, IBR, RAP (2026)

8 min read · May 2026
Advertisement · Google AdSense

The federal student loan repayment landscape changed significantly in 2026. The SAVE plan, which was available since 2023, has been replaced by the Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) starting July 1, 2026. If you have federal student loans, the plan you are on directly affects your monthly payment, total interest paid, and eligibility for forgiveness.

Here is what every plan means for your wallet, and how to decide which one is right for you.

Standard Plan: the financially optimal choice if you can afford it

The Standard Plan divides your balance into 120 equal monthly payments over 10 years. It is not fancy, but it minimizes total interest paid. Because you pay the loan off faster, less interest accumulates.

For a $35,000 loan at 6.39%, the standard monthly payment is about $393. Total cost over 10 years: roughly $47,000. On an extended 25-year plan, the same loan costs about $67,000 — $20,000 more in interest. If you can manage the standard payment, it almost always wins financially.

IBR (Income-Based Repayment): the safety net plan

IBR caps your monthly payment at 10% of discretionary income for new borrowers (15% for older loans). Discretionary income is roughly your adjusted gross income minus 150% of the federal poverty guideline for your family size. If your income is low, your payment can be very low — even zero.

The trade-off: payments are lower but the loan term extends to 20 or 25 years, meaning you pay more total interest. Any balance remaining at the end of the term is forgiven, though that forgiven amount may be taxable income under current law.

IBR makes sense when your debt is high relative to your income, or when you are pursuing Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF), which forgives the balance after 10 years of qualifying payments.

What is RAP? The new plan replacing SAVE (July 2026)

The Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) replaces SAVE starting July 1, 2026 for new borrowers. Here is how it works and how it differs from SAVE:

  • Payment structure: RAP payments are based on tiered percentages of your full adjusted gross income (AGI), not discretionary income. Rates range from 1% to 10% depending on your income level.
  • Minimum payment: $10 per month. Unlike SAVE, RAP eliminates the $0 payment option.
  • Interest subsidy: RAP keeps SAVE's interest protection — unpaid interest does not capitalize, so your balance does not grow when payments do not cover interest.
  • Forgiveness timeline: 30 years, which is longer than SAVE's 20-year timeline for undergrad borrowers.
  • PSLF compatibility: RAP payments count toward Public Service Loan Forgiveness, same as other IDR plans.

For borrowers with very low incomes who previously had $0 monthly payments under SAVE, RAP requires a $10 minimum. For most other borrowers, the practical effect depends on whether the AGI-based calculation produces a higher or lower payment than the old discretionary income formula.

PSLF: the most valuable program most people underutilize

Public Service Loan Forgiveness forgives your entire remaining federal loan balance after 120 qualifying payments (10 years) while working full-time for a government or qualifying nonprofit employer. There is no cap on the forgiveness amount, and the forgiven balance is tax-free under current law.

To qualify, you must be on an income-driven repayment plan (IBR, PAYE, or RAP), have Direct Loans, work full-time for an eligible employer, and submit an Employment Certification Form annually. Do not wait until year 10 to submit — submit it every year so issues can be corrected early.

For teachers, nurses, social workers, government employees, and nonprofit workers with significant loan balances, PSLF can be worth six figures in forgiveness. It is the most powerful federal loan benefit and one of the most underused.

How to choose the right plan

The decision comes down to a few questions. If you can comfortably afford the Standard Plan payment without financial stress, it saves you the most money long-term. If your payment-to-income ratio is high (loan payment above 10% of gross monthly income), an income-driven plan like IBR or RAP reduces financial pressure and keeps you in good standing. If you work or plan to work in public service or nonprofit, sign up for an IDR plan and pursue PSLF — the math often strongly favors it even if you could technically afford standard payments.
